After this positive experience, I spent a semester at the University of Montreal, taking courses on machine learning and natural language processing, in order to have theoretical knowledge and a stronger practical experience. I completed this formation by following a MOOC about deep learning, published by the MIT.
I then went back to Paris to validate my Master in Robotics by completing a 6-month internship at FieldBox.ai, where I explored computer vision algorithms to track industrial machines activity and operationalise these solutions through a web application.

Video-based industrial equipment activity tracking & operationalisation
FieldBox.ai, Paris, France  [internship]

February - August 2020

Explore computer vision algorithms to detect an industrial machine state, i.e. operating or turned off, and count the number of cycles operated. We first limited the study to unidirectional press machines, with the goal to later generalize the solution to other types of equipments.
I developed and compared different algorithms: the solution we adopted is based on the optical flow, i.e. computing points of interest in the frame and tracking them. This solution is easier to implement in a factory than a neural-network-based solution, like state-of-the-art Mask R-CNN (object instance segmentation) that requires a lot of labelled images to train the network.

I then worked with a team to operationalize these developed solutions. We developed a web application to which a remote device - like a smartphone or a raspberry pi placed in a factory, filming a machine - will send its video stream. We can then apply the developed solutions to the received real-time video stream.

Accelerometer-based Hand Gesture Recognition for Human-Robot Interaction
ISTeC Lab, Nottingham Trent University, England  [internship]

June - August 2019

Develop an innovative solution towards human-robot interaction using a wrist-worn tri-axial accelerometer. This was tackled as a three-fold gesture recognition problem with a gesture set including six different gestures, namely right, front, left, back, up and circle. I employed an adaptive segmentation technique (sliding window) to spot potential segments of interest within the signal. I led this project from the collection and manipulation of the data, until the comparison of different state-of-the-art classifiers, using Scikit-Learn and Pandas (Python).
Our scientific article was accepted and presented during the 2019 IEEE Symposium Series on Computational Intelligence, in Xiamen, China.
